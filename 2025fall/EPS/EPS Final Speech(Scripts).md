## II. Body

### 2.1 The Problem: The Pitfalls of Sticking to Traditional Career Expectations (Need - 指出问题：阐述遵循传统职业期待可能带来的职业倦怠、错失机遇等问题)

For decades, traditional career expectations have been like an invisible rule guiding our choices—prioritizing stability over passion, fetching reputation over personal fit, and acquiring the “approval of others” over our own happiness. 
But in today’s fast - evolving job market, this script is quickly becoming outdated, and following it blindly can lead to two major pitfalls.

**First, it often traps us in a cycle of career burnout.** 
Think about the elders around us: How many of them work in so-called “stable” jobs but complain every day about feeling unappreciated, bored, or drained? My uncle is a perfect example. He worked as a bank teller for 10 years, a job his parents hailed as an “iron rice bowl.” Every morning, he’d put on a suit with a forced smile, only to spend his days repeating the same tasks—counting money, verifying documents, and dealing with endless paperwork. 
He always dreamed of becoming a photographer, but he kept telling himself, “Stability comes first. I’ll pursue my passion later.”
Now, at 40, he finally mustered the courage to quit and switch careers. But he’s faced with countless obstacles: He lacks the professional experience that young photographers have, struggles to build connections in the industry, and can only take on low-paid amateur projects. Last month, he told me, “I regret not taking the risk when I was younger. Now, I feel like I’ve wasted years of my life on a job that never made me happy.”

**Second, insisting to traditional career paths blinds us to the booming opportunities in emerging fields, and thus deprives us of the ability to make major decisions in our life**. 
Just a decade ago, jobs like live-streaming operators, AI trainers, and sustainable consultants didn’t even exist. But today, these roles are in high demand, offering not just competitive salaries but also the chance to innovate and grow. On the flip side, many traditional jobs are shrinking due to technological advancements. 
Think about the AI trainers who teach robots to understand human kindness—did our childhood career lists even have that line? Think about the sustainability consultants who help companies heal the planet—was that ever called a “‘stable’ job”? Think about the metaverse designers building virtual worlds where people connect, create, even work—did we ever dream that could be a way to shape our lives?
With outdated views blinding us, we youth didn't even seek those new fields, just like  a robot that execute the outdated code, but never make our own decision to explore— That's horrible!

These aren’t isolated cases. According to “The 2024 Report on Chinese Young People's Career Choices” by China Youth Daily, 58% of young people who followed traditional career expectations, reported feeling “dissatisfied or extremely dissatisfied” with their jobs within the first three years. This is a clear sign that the old scripts isn’t working anymore—it’s time for us to write our own.

### 2.2 The Solution: How to Challenge Traditional Career Expectations Wisely (Satisfaction - 给出解决方案：从明确兴趣、调研规划、沟通家庭三方面说明如何明智挑战传统职业期待)

Challenging traditional career expectations doesn’t mean making impulsive, reckless choices. It means approaching our career decisions with intention, research, and open communication. Here are three practical steps to do it wisely.

**First, start by identifying your true interests and strengths—not what your parents think you’re good at, but what makes you absorbed in.** 
If you love creating content and connecting with people, maybe a career in digital marketing or content creation is right for you. If you’re passionate about solving environmental problems, roles in renewable energy or sustainable development could be a perfect fit. 
>My friend Lily, who majored in computer science, faced pressure from her parents to become a software engineer at a big tech company. But she realized that what she really loved was working with animals—she’d spend her weekends volunteering at an animal shelter and researching pet behavior. So, she took online courses in pet behavior training, interned at a reputable pet care center, and eventually became a freelance pet trainer. Today, she helps 10 - 15 pet owners every month solve issues like separation anxiety and aggressive behavior. She told me, “I used to think I had to choose between a ‘good job’ and a ‘happy job,’ but now I know they can be the same.”

**Second, do your research to turn your interests into a viable career plan.**
Many people worry that “jobs they love don’t pay well,” but this is a myth—especially in emerging fields. For example, digital artists who specialize in 3D modeling or game design can earn an average monthly salary of 15,000-25,000 yuan in major Chinese cities, which is 20% higher than the average entry-level salary in traditional industries like education or administration. 
Before making a decision, look up industry reports, talk to professionals in the field, and find out what skills you need to develop. If you’re interested in a career in short - video production, for instance, research the demand for short-video directors in your area, learn about the software and storytelling techniques required, and set a timeline for yourself—like “I’ll take a 3-month online course in video editing, then create 10 sample videos to build up my skills.”

**Third, communicate with your family openly and rationally—don’t see them as opponents, but as partners in your journey.** 
Most parents oppose non-traditional career choices not because they’re stubborn, but because they’re worried about your future. They grew up in a different era, where stability was the top priority, so they might not understand the opportunities in new fields. 
The key is to show them that your choice is not a “whim” but a well-thought-out plan.
>My roommate Tom wanted to work in the public welfare sector, but his parents were strongly against it. They said, “You’ll never make enough money to support yourself.” Instead of arguing with them, Tom invited his parents to visit the public welfare organization where he volunteered. They met the people he helped, saw the impact of his work, and even talked to his colleagues—who shared how the sector’s funding and career paths had grown in recent years. Slowly, his parents softened. Last month, when Tom got a full - time job at the organization, his mom even helped him pick out a new briefcase. That’s the power of communication: it turns fear into understanding, and doubt into support.

### 2.3 Visualization: What a Future of Challenging Traditional Career Expectations Looks Like (Visualization - 描绘愿景：展现挑战传统职业期待后，个人与社会层面的积极未来图景)

Now, let’s take a moment to imagine the future if we embrace this mindset—just transform “what’s always been done” into “what could be.”

**Picture this:** You wake up on a Monday morning, and instead of dreading work, you feel excited. You’re not stuck in a circle doing tasks that feel meaningless; you’re working on projects that light you up—maybe you’re creating a short film that tells a story you care about, or you’re helping a startup develop AI tools to protect the environment, or you’re training pets to make families happier. At the end of the day, you’re tired, but it’s a good tired—you know you made a difference, and you’re proud of what you do. You don’t have to lie to your parents about “liking your job”; you can tell them honestly, “I’m happy, and I’m successful in my own way.”

**And it’s not just about personal happiness—it’s about making the world a better place.** When young people challenge traditional career expectations, we bring new ideas, new skills, and new passion to fields that need them. A study by the World Economic Forum found that by 2030, 65% of the jobs that will be in high demand don’t exist yet. That means the future belongs to people who are willing to step outside the box—to create their own paths, instead of following the ones that were laid out for them.
