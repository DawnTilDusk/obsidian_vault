## Young People Should ChallengeTraditional Career Expectations

Good morning classmates and Professor Maija! Close your eyes for a second and imagine that:
at a dinner table, lights warm, your parents lean in with earnest voice: “Honey, pick a stable job
you like—doctor, lawyer, or civil servant. They'll make you not only  decent, but also secure and respectful.”
Now, open your eyes. Felt that stomach knot? Questions start hovering over our head: “What if
we hate hospitals or courts?” “What if those so-called ‘stable’ feels empty?” “Is ‘decent’ only
what seniors told us?”
We’ve all been there—trapped between the love of our parents and the quiet voice of our own
desires.

We’ll spend 90,000 hours at work—nearly one third of our lives. So these aren’t over-thoughts;
they’re about whether we’ll live our own life, not just repeating someone else’s. Today, let’s
turn the doubt to clarity: Young people must courageously challenge traditional career expectations.


For decades, traditional career expectations have been like invisible rules guiding us—prioritize
stability over passion, prefer seniors' approval rather than ourpersonal happiness. But in today’s
fast-evolving job market, those are quickly becoming outdated. Blindly following them  is just like digging two pitfalls in front of us:

Career burnout comes first. （点ppt）
My uncle was a bank teller for 10 years, which his parents hailed
as “iron rice bowl”. His daily tasks are just forced smiles and a mountain of charts to fill, he always
dreaming of photography but delaying it over and over again. At
  40,  He was finally fed up with it and quit the job to make a
change with no experience, low-paid and amateur gears.  It's clear to all of us that“He had wasted years on unhappiness,” 

Then arrives  another pitfall：missing emerging opportunities. 
A decade ago, AI trainers, sustainability consultants or metaverse designers didn’t even exist— but now they’re in high demand
Meanwhile, traditional jobs shrink: bank tellers have dropped 35% over the past 5 years in China, replaced by
tech. Stuck in old views, we lose decision-making skills to explore—like my classmate Alex who was
forced into finance by parents, now lost:   He told me once“ honestly I never learned to choose what I want.”

Data also backs this:  According to China Youth Daily’s 2024 report，nearly  58% of the youth who followed tradition felt “dissatisfied” with jobs in the first 3 years. So, the old scripts fails—we need to explore a new one.


However，challenging tradition isn’t reckless—it’s intentional. Here are 3 simple steps for you:
First, follow your absorption: Not your parents told you what you’re good at ,” but what makes you lose track of time. Just like the founder of Unitree Robitics, Wangxingxing who dives into the fields of robot by passion from a young age: Happiness and good jobs can come at the same time  if we follow our hearts.

Second, Research to build a plan: “new jobs don’t pay” is just a myth. What we need to do is to find . It is reported that digital artists in the game "Black Myth: Wukong" earns nearly 20 thousands per month, its
much much higher than  most traditional jobs at an entry level. So pick up an interest like short-video. take a 3-month editing course, make 10 sample videos.  You are taking steps to become an expert.

Third, talk to parents as partners: They oppose not to be stubborn, but to worry. My parents used to push me to computer science. But as I manifest my love for engineering and explain my plan for my future, they even actively support me to create some small mechanical works.

You can Imagine this future: On Monday mornings, you wake excited with no awful work. You’re making
short videos you care about, building AI for the environment. And at the end of the day， Tired but proud, you can tell your parents honestly: “I’m fulfilling for what I am doing.”
And This picture will be not just personal: we bring new passion to new fields. According to the World Economic Forum, a half of 2030’s high-demand jobs don’t even exist—and this future belongs to us, the rule-breakers as well as the rule-makers.

So, let’s call back the dinner table: Remember the doubt? Today, here’s our answer—firm and
specific: We don’t settle for the past’s “only-decent choices.” We challenge old stereotypes and
seek for our own devotion.
Just start small this week: Spend 30 minutes writing what you love, research one career tied to
it, and talk to parents with sincerity.
We don’t need all the answers at once—just go for it! Those 90,000 working hours do matter to our happiness.
We will not follow the future— instead， we create it.
Thank you.
