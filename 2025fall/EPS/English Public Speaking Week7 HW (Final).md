As a freshman who’d just climbed out of high school’s "study cage"—only to land in another —— major involution in university. Every night, my friends lit the midnight with his lamp glowing over textbooks. Group chats blew up with messages like "Who finished 10 extra math exercises?" 
I forced myself to keep up, even though my chest felt as tight as a squeezed sponge. Every day, I’d cross "passion" off my to-do list, telling myself, "That’s a waste of time—focus on your grades."

But that day changed everything. I walked past the geek club room and heard loud cheers. Curious, I peeked in. Students were huddled around a drone, twisting its propellers. It looked like a lopsided frog—ugly but cute. Soon the drone lifted off, it's graceful flight instantly arrests my eyes. I felt, for the first time, a window opening in my stuffy heart.

After that, I started sneaking to club meetings after homework. At first, I always messed up the circuit board—like a blind guy trying to put a puzzle together with some wrong pieces. But kind seniors stayed late to help with. We spent weekends debugging, even if it meant skipping text-book review sessions.
Slowly, I found myself stopped checking how many pages others had read, stopped measuring myself against their to-do lists. When my drone got through an obstacle, that happiness was bigger than any grades.

For the first time again, I didn’t feel guilty for so-called "falling behind."

Now I get it: involution is just an endless loop—we run as fast as we can, but never arrive anywhere. We don’t have to "keep up" to be good enough  because finding what makes us happy isn’t a distraction; but the fuel that keeps us going.
