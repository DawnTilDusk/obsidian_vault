Good morning classmates and Professor Maija! Close your eyes for a second and imagine that: at a dinner table, lights warm, your parents lean in with sincere voice: “Honey, pick a stable job you like—doctor, lawyer, or civil servant. They'll make you decent, secure and respectful.”​

Now, open your eyes. Felt that stomach knot? Questions start hovering over our head: “What if we hate hospitals or courts?” “What if those so-called ‘stable’ feels empty?” “Is ‘decent’ only what seniors say?”​

We’ve all been there—trapped between the love of our parents and the quiet voice of our own desires. 

We’ll spend 90,000 hours at work—nearly one third of our lives. So these aren’t over-thoughts; they’re about whether we’ll live our own life, not just repeating someone else’s. **Today, let’s turn the doubt to clarity: Young people must courageously challenge traditional career expectations.​**

---
For decades, traditional career expectations have been like invisible rules guiding us—prioritize stability over passion, prefer others' approval rather than individual happiness. However, I had to say: **Those outdated rules dug two big traps for you!**

**Career burnout comes first.** My uncle was a bank teller for 10 years, which his parents hailed as “iron rice bowl”. His daily tasks are just forced smiles and repetitive tasks, he always dreaming of photography but delaying it over and over again. Now 40, he quit the job to make a change with no experience, low-paid and amateur gears. “I wasted years on unhappiness,” he said.​

**Then arrives missing emerging opportunities.** A decade ago, AI trainers, sustainability consultants or metaverse designers didn’t even exist— but now they’re in high demand. Meanwhile, traditional jobs shrink: bank tellers dropped 35% in 5 years in China, replaced by tech. Stuck in old views, we lose decision-making skills too—like my classmate Alex who was forced into finance by parents, now lost: “I never learned to choose what I want.”​

Data backs this: China Youth Daily’s 2024 report shows 58% of youth who followed tradition felt “dissatisfied” with jobs in 3 years. So, the old scripts fails—we need a explore new ones.​

---
Challenging tradition isn’t reckless—it’s intentional. Here are 3 simple steps:​

**First, follow your absorption: Not parents’ “you’re good at this,” but what makes you lose track of time.** Just like the founder of Unitree Robitics, Wangxingxing who dives into the fields of robot from a young age: Happy and good jobs can be the same.

**Second, Research to build a plan: “Loved jobs don’t pay” is a myth.** It is reported that digital artists in the game *"Black Myth: Wukong"* earns nearly 20 thousands per month, which is 20% higher than traditional entry-level jobs. So pick one interest like short-video production? Take a 3-month editing course, make 10 sample videos.​

**Third, talk to parents as partners: They oppose not to be stubborn, but to worry.** My parents used to push me to computer science. But as I manifest my love for engineering and explain my plan for my future, they even actively support me to create some small works.

---

Imagine this future: On Monday mornings, you wake excited with no awful work. You’re making short videos you care about, building AI for the environment. Tired but proud, you can tell parents honestly: “I’m fulfilling.”​

This picture will be not just personal: we bring new passion to new fields. According to the World Economic Forum, 65% of 2030’s high-demand jobs don’t exist yet—this future belongs to us, the rule-breakers as well as the rule-makers.​

***

So, let’s call back the dinner table: Remember the doubt? Today, here’s our answer—firm and specific: We don’t settle for the past’s “only-decent choices.” We challenge old stereotypes and seek our own fortune.​
